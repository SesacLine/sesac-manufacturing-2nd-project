WAFER MAP MORPHOLOGY HEURISTICS - EXPERT FIELD NOTES

These are the rules of thumb a senior yield engineer uses to read a wafer map by
its spatial signature alone - the geometric form, the morphology, and the shape
of the failing-die distribution - before the defect class is even named. Each
heuristic maps an observed shape, its location on the wafer, and its morphology
to the process step that should be suspected first. They are meant to be used
when the pattern class is uncertain or unknown: the shape itself, not the class
label, narrows down where to look.

For each shape we also record its morphology in three ways, because the same
shape can look very different: how dense the failing dies are (dense, moderate,
or sparse), whether the shape is continuous or broken (continuous, intermittent,
or discontinuous), and how far it wraps around the wafer (full circumference,
partial, or a specific clock direction). The morphology sharpens the reading but
does not override the shape-to-step mapping; treat it as supporting evidence.

Continuous ring at the wafer edge
When the failing dies form a dense, continuous, circular ring that wraps the full
circumference of the wafer edge, the shape most commonly points to the ETCH step.
This annular ring at the edge reflects etch-rate nonuniformity that intensifies
toward the wafer perimeter, so the etch chamber is the first place to look. The
same dense, continuous, full-circumference edge ring can also arise in the DEPO
step, where thin-film deposition thins out along the wafer edge. On seeing a
dense continuous ring around the whole edge, suspect ETCH first and DEPO second.

Broken arc on one side of the edge
When the ring is not closed but appears as a sparse, broken, discontinuous arc
covering only part of the edge - for example concentrated around the 5, 6, and 7
o'clock positions on the lower edge rather than the full circumference - the shape
points to the CMP step. This partial, one-sided edge arc reflects asymmetric edge
handling or localized over-polish during chemical mechanical polishing, which
attacks one side of the wafer edge more than the rest.

Donut ring offset toward mid-radius
When the failing dies form a dense, continuous donut band that is closed all the
way around but sits at mid-radius - an annular ring offset inward from the
perimeter, away from both the center and the edge - the shape commonly points to
the DEPO step, where a radial deposition-rate band forms at an intermediate
radius. This dense, continuous, full-circumference mid-radius ring can also
reflect the CMP step, where zoned polishing pressure over-polishes an annular
band partway out from the center.

Concentrated cluster at the center
When the failing dies form a dense, solid, continuous concentrated cluster - a
tight blob at the geometric center of the wafer, the classic bulls-eye - the
shape typically points to the DEPO step. A dense center-weighted cluster reflects
deposition nonuniformity that peaks at the wafer center, so the deposition
chamber is the first suspect. The cluster is solid rather than ring-shaped, so it
has no circumferential direction.

Linear streak across the wafer
When the failing dies form a thin, continuous, low-density linear streak - an
elongated directional line cutting across the wafer with no fixed zone - the shape
most commonly points to the CMP step. The sparse continuous streak reflects
mechanical abrasion during chemical mechanical polishing: the polish pad, or a
hard particle trapped under it, gouges a thin continuous line into the wafer
surface. A thin continuous linear streak can also arise in the CLEAN step, where
wafer handling or excessive megasonic energy scratches the surface along a line.

Global wafer-wide distribution
When the failing dies are spread across the entire wafer with a diffuse,
moderate-density, continuous coverage - a global, wafer-wide signature that
touches the whole surface in every direction - the shape commonly points to the
CLEAN step, where a chemical bath or particle excursion contaminates the entire
wafer. This moderate, continuous, full-wafer form can also reflect the DEPO step,
where a whole-wafer film nonuniformity degrades every die at once.

Random scattered dies
When the failing dies are sparse, discontinuous, and randomly scattered, with no
geometric structure and no preferred location or direction, the shape commonly
points to the CLEAN step. The sparse, broken, random scatter reflects random
particulate contamination landing on the wafer surface during cleaning. A sparse
random scatter rarely indicates a systematic process-parameter drift; it points
to particle control in the CLEAN step first.
